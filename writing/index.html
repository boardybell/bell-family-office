A Note on the Founding of the Firm

By Bond Bell · Fort Wayne, Indiana

[DATE TO BE SET]

Bell Family Office is founded because a subset of the clients of Bond Bell & Co require a practice that is structurally different from conventional advisory work.

For most of my working life I have watched families of real means receive advice from institutions that had, structurally, no capacity to know them. The private bank has its product shelf. The wirehouse has its quota. The independent advisor has fewer conflicts but often less depth, and the true family office, as it exists at the Rockefeller scale, is a structure available only to families large enough to capitalize one themselves.

The families this firm works with sit between these categories. Their capital is real but not institutional in scale. It was built, in most cases, recently and rapidly, through specific kinds of work: the founding of technology companies, careers in entertainment, professional athletic careers and their agencies, and the operating businesses that surround these industries. The planning questions these families face are specific to how their capital was produced. How is a founder's pre-exit estate structured for a liquidity event that may or may not occur at a certain valuation. How is an athlete's short earning window translated into capital that must last a long adult life. How is a writer's royalty stream, a director's back-end participation, or an agency's book of relationships passed to heirs who may or may not want any of it. How is a technology founder's second company positioned when the first one created enough wealth that the second is being built for reasons other than money. These are not conventional wealth management problems. They require judgment, structure, and careful drafting by people who understand the industries the wealth came from.

Bond Bell & Co was established to work on problems of this shape. Bell Family Office is the tier of that practice reserved for families whose engagements require the full scope: the drafting, the coordination of counsel, the management of capital across generations, and the direct attention of the principal. The tier formalizes what was already the practice with those clients, so that the work has a name, a discipline, and a clear set of terms.

Most clients are based in Los Angeles, Silicon Valley and the Bay Area, Seattle, Austin, or New York. The firm itself operates from Fort Wayne, Indiana. It is where I live. I travel to clients; the practice is built around being present for the meetings that matter, in the cities where those meetings happen. The choice of Fort Wayne as the firm's base is personal rather than strategic, but it has produced a structural consequence I did not initially plan for. A firm located outside the major financial centers is free of some of the rhythms and pressures that shape coastal professional services. The pace of the work is set by the clients' actual situations, not by the quarterly cadence of the industry or the noise of being in proximity to everyone else doing similar work. Whether that is an advantage is a judgment each client will make for themselves. I have found it useful.

The family office is small by design. I work with each client directly. When complex tax questions arise, I coordinate with counsel whom I have known for many years. When a client's situation requires specialized investment analysis that exceeds the firm's capacity, I refer the matter to colleagues who specialize in it and whom I trust to do the work carefully. The firm does not attempt to do everything. A family office that claims to do everything usually does most things poorly.

The family office charges an annual retainer rather than a percentage of assets. The retainer is set by the complexity of the engagement, not by the size of the portfolio. This structure removes the most common conflict in the category, which is that firms paid on assets are paid more to advise a family to hold assets than to distribute them, even when distribution would be correct. The retainer structure also clarifies what the work actually is. Clients pay for drafting, coordination, and attention, which are the services actually rendered.

There is one thing I want a reader of this page to understand, if they understand nothing else. The work of this tier is not investment management in the conventional sense. Portfolios are managed, and they are managed carefully. But the portfolio is the easier half of the engagement. The harder half is the quiet work of drafting, coordinating counsel, sitting through the family meetings, and making sure that the instruments in place today will still function when the family is different in ways no one can currently predict. That work is the reason the tier exists. It is also the reason a retainer is the honest way to bill for it.

The family office does not solicit new clients. Most new engagements begin through an introduction, either from an existing client, as the practice grows, or from a member of counsel. This is not a marketing posture. It is how a practice of this kind is built. Readers who find the firm through other means are welcome to write; the email address is below.

The tier is not for everyone. It is for families who think in decades rather than quarters, who want one advisor who knows the whole picture, and who are willing to do the slow work of being known by their advisor in return. If that sounds like the kind of practice you have been looking for, the firm can be reached at correspondence@bellfamilyoffice.com.

A Misunderstanding About the Work

A common misconception and why it persists.

15 April 2026

A common misunderstanding about what a family office does, repeated in conversations with prospective clients several times in the past year:

The misunderstanding is that the family office's primary work is investment management, and that the other services the family office offers are secondary or ancillary to the investments.

This is reversed.

For most families at the size the firm serves, investment management is the simpler part of the work. There are excellent outside managers for every reasonable asset class, and the job of selecting from among them is not especially mysterious once a few basic principles are in place. It takes time, it takes attention, it takes willingness to fire managers when they deserve firing, but it is not the hard part.

The hard part is everything else: the drafting and revision of instruments, the coordination of counsel, the navigation of family transitions, the preparation of heirs, the structuring of philanthropy, the management of relationships with accountants and trustees and attorneys across multiple jurisdictions, the careful attention to the compounding decisions that shape the family's situation across decades. These are the activities that distinguish a family office from a brokerage or a wealth manager, and they are the activities that justify the fee structure.

The misunderstanding persists because the category markets itself as primarily about investment returns. This is a residue of the retail wealth management industry, where performance is the main thing a firm can advertise. At the scale the firm serves, performance is not the main thing. The main thing is the coordination and continuity that the family cannot easily provide for itself.

A family that arrives at the firm expecting investment management primarily is not a family the firm can serve well. A family that arrives understanding that investment management is one piece of a larger practice is a family the firm can work with over decades.

The conversation at the first meeting often circles this misunderstanding. I try to name it early.

A Letter on Capital That Exceeds the Heirs’ Appetite

The particular difficulty of the third generation, and why planning for it must begin in the first.

12 April 2026

To the reader who is thinking about the next generation:

There is a pattern in family capital that every multi-generational family eventually encounters, and that is the subject of this letter.

The pattern is this. Capital accumulates in the first generation through work. The first generation knows how the money was made because they were there when it was made. The second generation inherits the money and usually has a working relationship to it, in the sense that they grew up watching it being earned or that they came of age when the family's situation was still being shaped by the work that produced the capital. The third generation does not have this relationship. For the third generation, the capital is a condition of birth rather than a result of effort.

This is not a moral observation. It is a structural one. The third generation's situation is not of its own making. The capital arrived before the third generation could have any opinion about it. The question is what the capital does, by the time it reaches generations that did not produce it.

Most of what is usually written about this question is unhelpful. The common framing is that the third generation will either fail to respect the capital, or will be corrupted by it, or will fight over it, or will fritter it away. These outcomes are described with a moralistic tone that is not especially useful for planning, and that tends to flatter the first generation at the expense of the third.

A more useful framing, I think, is that the capital by the third generation usually exceeds the family's real appetite for it, and the question is what to do with the excess.

By "appetite," I do not mean consumption preferences. I mean the capital the family can productively absorb into its life, including its work, its philanthropy, its housing, its children's education, and the normal activities that wealth can fund. For most families of the size the firm serves, this appetite is finite. It does not grow without limit as the capital grows. By the third generation, the capital has usually compounded past the level the family can absorb, and the excess must go somewhere.

The excess goes, in my observation, to one of four destinations. It is given away, through serious philanthropy. It is placed in structures that keep it out of reach, through trusts designed to last many generations. It is distributed to the heirs in increments large enough to change their lives, in which case the capital typically dissipates within one or two further generations. Or it accumulates into amounts that require professional institutional management as if the family were a foundation, which it effectively becomes.

Each of these destinations is legitimate. Each requires planning that must begin well before the capital reaches the third generation. Philanthropy requires vehicles and governance that take years to establish. Long-term trusts require drafting that must contemplate conditions the first generation cannot foresee. Distribution to heirs requires preparing those heirs for the responsibility. Institutional management requires the family to decide what the capital is for, because institutional management is not free of direction.

The planning, in other words, must begin in the first generation, for a condition that will arrive two or three generations later.

This is where a great deal of wealth management goes wrong. The first generation, in many cases, does not want to think about the third generation's problem. They are focused on their own work, their own children, their own plans. The question of what happens when the capital exceeds the family's appetite feels distant and hypothetical. It is not. It is almost certain. A family of means who reaches the third generation without having planned for it will find the planning forced on them, by estate pressures, by family conflict, or by the simple accumulation of capital beyond the family's ability to manage it informally.

The role of a family office, I think, is to raise this question early and to return to it regularly across the life of the engagement. Not as a pitch, not as an occasion for billable work, but as a structural feature of the conversation. What happens when the capital exceeds the family's appetite? Where does the excess go? Who decides? These are the questions that define the family's long-term situation, and they are the questions that most wealth management conversations do not raise.

With regards,

Bond Bell

Fort Wayne, Indiana

On the Relationship with Counsel

A family office that coordinates counsel well is doing the single most valuable thing the tier offers. A family office that coordinates counsel poorly is worse than having no family office at all.

8 April 2026

A family with real assets will typically work with a dozen or more professional advisors across their lives: trust attorneys, estate attorneys, tax counsel, transaction counsel, accounting firms, investment advisors, insurance specialists, business attorneys, real estate counsel, and depending on the family's situation, specialists in other areas. Each of these professionals is an expert in a specific field. None of them is tasked with understanding the family's situation as a whole.

The family office's central role, in my view, is to be the party that holds the whole picture and coordinates the specialists.

This is a claim that deserves to be examined, because if it is true, it reshapes how the firm's work should be understood.

Consider what happens when a family has no coordinator. An estate attorney drafts a trust that accomplishes specific tax objectives. Six months later, a tax attorney restructures an entity in a way that is efficient for tax purposes but creates a conflict with the trust structure. Two years later, a transaction attorney advises on the sale of an asset in a manner that is consistent with local law but does not fit the governance structure the family had settled on the previous year. Each of these advisors did their work competently. The result is that the family has a set of structures that do not fit together, and the family will discover this over time, usually at the moment when the misfit matters most.

With a coordinator, this does not happen. The estate attorney and the tax attorney are both briefed by the family office, which understands the family's situation in enough detail to identify potential conflicts before they are baked in. The transaction attorney does not work on the sale without the family office being in the loop, because the family office is the standing party that knows what other decisions the sale will affect. The specialists remain specialists. They do their work at a higher standard because they are briefed well and because their work is being fitted into a consistent whole. The family benefits from the expertise of each specialist without suffering the fragmentation that would otherwise result.

The coordinator role is simple to describe and hard to do.

It is hard because the coordinator must have enough technical knowledge in each domain to know when a specialist is being sloppy or when two specialists' work is in tension. The coordinator does not need to be a trust attorney to work with trust counsel effectively, but the coordinator needs to know enough trust law to ask the right questions. The same is true for tax, for transaction work, for governance, for investment manager selection. A coordinator who does not have this breadth of knowledge is just a file clerk. A coordinator who has it is a peer to the specialists and can speak with them in the dialect of each field.

It is also hard because the coordinator must have the standing to push back on specialists when their recommendations are wrong. A specialist usually outranks a generalist in any single domain. A specialist who has drafted a trust a thousand times will, at first blush, know more about trust drafting than a family office that has worked with perhaps two dozen. The family office has to earn the right to push back by bringing something the specialist does not have: the knowledge of the family's situation in its full context. When the family office says "this clause will conflict with the operating agreement the family signed last year, here is the relevant passage," the specialist listens. When the family office says "I think this clause should be different," without the specific reason, the specialist does not. Earning the standing to be heard is part of the slow work the previous essay described.

The coordinator relationship requires selecting the specialists carefully, at the front end. The firm maintains working relationships with a small network of counsel: trust and estate attorneys in Indiana and several other states where clients have principal residences, tax counsel at two firms, a valuation firm, a transactional attorney in Chicago, an insurance specialist whose work I have learned to trust. When a new matter arises, I know which specialist to bring in, and the specialist knows that the referral comes with the expectation that the work will be done to a particular standard. The network is mutual. The specialists occasionally refer work to the firm that falls within our scope.

Families who find the firm useful tend to describe this coordination function as the most valuable thing the firm provides. They describe it as "having one number to call," but what they mean is something more: they mean that there is a party who holds the whole picture and can ensure the specialists' work fits together. This is a thing a family can provide for itself, if the family has the time and the expertise. Most do not. The firm provides it on their behalf.

A firm that claims to coordinate counsel but does not actually do it well produces worse outcomes than no coordination at all, because the specialists rely on the coordinator to have done the work of alignment, and when the coordinator has not, the specialists' work proceeds in parallel without the alignment they assumed was happening. I have seen this failure mode in several families who arrived at the firm from other offices. Recovering from it is not simple.

Coordination, done well, is most of what the family office is paid to do.

From the Office, Fort Wayne

A small observation from the early weeks of the practice.

1 April 2026

A Tuesday morning in early spring.

I am sitting with a draft of a trust instrument for a family in the second generation. The document is thirty-seven pages. The attorney who will finalize it is a woman in Indianapolis I have worked with for eleven years. She will produce the final version in about ten days, after which it will move to counsel in the client's state for local review. There are three more versions of this document ahead. None of them will be significantly different from the draft I am looking at now. The differences will be small, technical, and accumulated through several rounds of attention.

The work of this morning is to read the draft slowly. To mark questions in the margin. To note places where the document reflects something the family told me last fall that has since changed, and to flag those places for the family's review before counsel finalizes. This work takes about four hours. It will not appear in any report. The client will not be aware of it as a distinct activity. The only evidence of it will be that the final document, when it arrives, will be free of the errors that would have been introduced if the work had not been done.

This is what the practice looks like most days. Documents are read. Questions are noted. Small errors are prevented. Occasionally a small error is allowed through because the cost of fixing it exceeds the cost of the error itself, but this decision is made consciously.

There is a view of financial services in which this is not what a senior advisor does. In that view, senior advisors do higher-value work, and document review is delegated to juniors or paralegals. I do not hold that view. The document is the whole point. A family office that does not read documents carefully is a family office that is letting the document work happen elsewhere, which means the firm does not know what is actually in the documents that govern its clients' affairs. This is a poor way to practice.

The office is quiet this morning. Fort Wayne is quiet in general. The quiet is useful for this kind of work.

A Letter on the Family Business

When a family holds an operating business, the family office has a specific and narrow role to play.

17 March 2026

To the reader whose family owns an operating business:

The single largest asset in many of the families the firm serves is not a portfolio but an operating business. The business is often the source of the family's wealth, the center of its identity, and the locus of its most difficult decisions. Whether to keep it, whether to sell it, whether to bring in professional management, whether to transfer it to the next generation, whether to take on outside investors: these are among the most consequential questions the family will face, and the family office has a specific role to play in them.

The specific role is not to run the business. It is not to select the investment bankers who might help sell it. It is not to become the trusted operator if professional management is needed. The role is smaller and more specific.

The firm's role with respect to a family-owned business is threefold.

The first role is to hold the business in the context of the family's overall situation. A family with a forty-million-dollar operating business and eight million dollars in liquid assets is a different family than one with fifteen million dollars in the business and thirty-five million dollars outside. The appropriate handling of the business depends on its role in the broader picture. The firm thinks about this picture continuously, not just when a decision is pending.

The second role is to coordinate the specialists who will be needed when the business reaches an inflection point. Sale, succession, recapitalization, major capital expenditure, generational transition: each of these calls for counsel the firm does not employ. Investment bankers, transaction attorneys, forensic accountants, valuation specialists, succession consultants. The firm's job is to understand the family's situation well enough to brief these specialists properly, to filter their recommendations through what we know about the family, and to ensure the specialists are serving the family rather than their own fees.

The third role is to hold the long view of the business across decades. Specialists come and go. Investment bankers work on transactions and then disappear. Transaction attorneys close deals and move to the next client. The family office is the party that remembers what was decided in 2014 and why, and why the structure chosen at that time still makes sense or no longer does. The continuity of institutional memory about the business is one of the most valuable things a family office provides.

What the firm does not do, explicitly: the firm does not recommend whether to sell. That decision is the family's. The firm can lay out the considerations, describe what happens if the family sells and what happens if it does not, and help the family think through the decision. The firm does not advocate. Families who want an advisor who will push them toward or away from sale are looking for a different kind of counsel.

What the firm does not do, also explicitly: the firm does not serve as interim management. A family whose operating business needs professional management should hire professional management, and should do so through a proper search process. The family office can help run that search or can coordinate with a search firm that runs it. The family office cannot itself fill the role.

Families who understand these limits tend to find the firm more useful, not less. The limits clarify what is actually being offered, which is the slow continuous attention to the business as part of the family's broader situation, over long periods of time.

With regards,

Bond Bell

Fort Wayne, Indiana

A Note on the 2025 Estate Tax Changes

Brief observations on the current environment and what it suggests for families with meaningful estates.

3 March 2026

The federal estate tax exemption, which doubled in 2017 and was scheduled to sunset at the end of 2025, was made permanent in the One Big Beautiful Bill Act of 2025 at an adjusted level of approximately fifteen million dollars per individual (thirty million per couple) for deaths after 2025, with continued inflation indexing.

Before the bill, families in the approximately six-to-fifteen-million-dollar range had been doing substantial planning on the assumption that the exemption would return to roughly seven million per individual at the end of 2025. Much of that planning is now unnecessary or at least no longer urgent.

Two observations, offered without recommendation.

First, families who completed gifting strategies in 2024 or early 2025 under the prior sunset assumption should not treat those actions as mistakes. The exemption could change again. Congress's willingness to revisit the number at future budget reconciliation moments is not zero, and a family that used exemption already has locked in certainty about at least that portion of the transfer.

Second, the effective estate tax threshold for a married couple is now high enough that many families who had been planning around estate tax minimization should reconsider what they are actually planning for. Above fifteen million dollars per spouse, the planning questions remain the same. Below it, the dominant questions become asset protection, generational governance, and the preparation of heirs, rather than tax minimization. These are different questions and they call for different instruments.

A family whose estate planning was primarily driven by an assumption of lower exemption numbers should probably review its documents with counsel. The review is not urgent. It should happen over the course of the year ahead.

This is an observation, not advice. Specific decisions are the province of specific conversations with counsel and advisors who know the family's situation in detail.

On the Slow Work

Much of the practice is the coordination of counsel, the reading of documents, and the quiet attendance at meetings. This is what the firm is actually paid to do.

18 February 2026

If a reader were to follow me through a typical week at the firm, the work would look unlike what the category usually presents itself as doing.

There would be no trading. There would be one or two conversations with clients, usually by phone, often about matters that are not urgent and would not have warranted the call if we did not have the standing habit of checking in. There would be several hours reading documents: trust instruments under revision by counsel, tax returns prepared by outside accountants, operating agreements for family LLCs, drafts of letters between family members about business decisions. There would be a meeting or two with clients' attorneys. There would be a phone call with an accountant about a specific tax position and whether the position is correct. There would be research into a particular investment manager a client is considering, which would produce a memo recommending against the manager, which would save the client from making an allocation they would have regretted within three years.

This is the work, and it is slow.

The slow work is the work the category talks about least, because it is difficult to market. A firm cannot easily advertise "we read your documents carefully" in a way that sounds like a service. A firm cannot put "we attend your quarterly family meetings and take careful notes" in a marketing deck without making the work sound diminished by the description. But the slow work is what the retainer is paying for. It is the work that separates a family office from a brokerage relationship.

There is a particular kind of value in attending a family meeting and not speaking for the first hour. The advisor who speaks first is establishing a posture of authority. The advisor who listens first is establishing a posture of attention. In a multi-generational family meeting, the second posture is almost always more useful. The advisor learns how the family talks to itself, which members are trusted, which decisions are still being contested, and which tensions are being avoided. None of this would be visible to an advisor who arrived with an agenda and drove through it.

The slow work is also how the advisor earns the right to make hard recommendations when they are needed. When the eventual hard recommendation comes — a trust should be reformed, a business should be sold, a family member should be asked to step back from a role they are not performing well in — the recommendation is credible because the advisor has been present for long enough to have earned the credibility. A recommendation made in the first year of an engagement carries less weight than the same recommendation in the fifth year. The weight is accumulated in the hours of listening and document-reading and unglamorous attention.

I have come to think of this accumulation as a kind of capital in its own right. It is not financial capital. It is the capital of having paid attention. It compounds the same way financial capital compounds: slowly, usefully, and with occasional large payoffs at unpredictable moments. A family that has been worked with carefully for a decade has a reservoir of this attention-capital that the advisor can draw on when the family hits a hard decision. A family that has worked with its advisor for six months has no such reservoir.

This is the real case for the long engagement. Not that the advisor becomes more efficient over time, though they do. Not that the fee structure amortizes better, though it does. The real case is that the slow work produces a kind of knowledge about a specific family that cannot be produced by any other means, and when that knowledge is needed, usually at a moment of family crisis or transition, nothing else substitutes for it.

The firm's practice is designed to accumulate this knowledge. The retainer structure protects the time required. The small client count ensures the principal can hold all of it in mind at once. The refusal of unnecessary transactions prevents the kind of artificial activity that would displace the slow attention.

A reader who is considering the firm should know that what they are paying for is largely invisible. It does not show up in statements. It does not produce year-end reports of trades executed or allocations rebalanced. What it produces is a different kind of result: decisions made at moments of real stake, informed by years of accumulated attention, that work well for the family across decades.

Families who understand this describe the firm's work in terms that do not fit the category's marketing vocabulary. They say things like "they know us." They say "they remember what we decided in 2023 and why." They say "when we call, we are not explaining from scratch."

That is the product. The slow work is how it is made.

A Letter on the Limits of the Firm

What the firm does not do, and why refusing certain work is itself a form of service.

4 February 2026

To the reader who is evaluating the firm:

A family office that claims to do everything usually does most things poorly. This is the governing constraint of the practice, and it produces a list of things the firm does not do, which I will state plainly.

The firm does not make direct investments in private companies. When a client's situation calls for private equity analysis, I refer the work to a firm that specializes in it. We have relationships with two such firms and the referral is without compensation in either direction.

The firm does not provide direct tax preparation. We coordinate with tax counsel and we read the returns carefully when they arrive. We do not prepare them. Preparing returns is a different practice with different licensing and different insurance, and I do not believe the firm would do it at the standard its clients deserve.

The firm does not manage retirement accounts below one million dollars. This is not a judgment about the accounts or the clients who hold them. It is a judgment about the firm. Small retirement accounts require a different service model, often a different regulatory structure, and a different set of tools than what the firm uses for its larger engagements. Clients with smaller retirement accounts are better served by firms that specialize in that work.

The firm does not advise on the sale of private operating businesses above approximately fifty million dollars in enterprise value. At that size the work becomes genuinely specialized, and specialist investment banks do it better than a family office can. Below that threshold, the firm will often be involved, particularly if the owner is also a client whose broader affairs we coordinate.

The firm does not practice law. I draft trust instruments in close consultation with trust counsel, who is always involved and always signs the final document. The firm's role is to understand the family's situation well enough to brief counsel thoroughly and to produce an early draft that counsel can refine. The line between substantive drafting and the unauthorized practice of law matters, and the firm stays on the correct side of it.

The firm does not offer its own investment products. We do not have a model portfolio, a proprietary fund, a structured product, or a branded strategy. Every investment recommendation the firm makes is to buy from an outside manager, and the firm receives no compensation from those managers.

I state these limits not as a marketing posture but as an operational description. A reader who is considering the firm should know what it will and will not do. The alternative, a firm that claims to do everything and then quietly refers out what it cannot do, is a worse arrangement for the client. The client pays for capabilities the firm does not have, and the referrals happen without the client's clear understanding of why.

On Reading the Statement

A small discipline that most clients do not practice and most advisors do not encourage.

22 January 2026

Most clients do not read their monthly or quarterly investment statements carefully. This is true at all levels of sophistication and all sizes of portfolio. The statements are long, the fees are embedded, the comparisons are selective, and the font is usually small. The advisor knows the client does not read the statement. Sometimes the advisor has built the statement with this knowledge in mind.

I have come to believe that reading the statement carefully, with the client, is one of the most important things an advisor can do. It takes ninety minutes. It is not glamorous work. The conversation reveals fees the client did not know were being charged, positions the client did not know they owned, and structural choices the client did not know had been made on their behalf. Most of what is revealed is not wrong. Some of it is. The reading surfaces both.

I do this once a year with every client. It is not mentioned on the firm's page of services, because services are not really the frame. It is part of what it means to be paying attention.

On the Retainer

Why the firm charges a flat annual fee rather than a percentage of assets, and what that structure reveals about the work.

8 January 2026

Almost every firm in the wealth management category charges a percentage of assets under management. This has been the industry-standard fee structure for forty years. It is not the structure we use. The reason we do not use it is worth explaining, because the fee structure is not a minor operational choice. It is a statement about what the firm is paid to do.

The AUM fee has two problems. The first is a conflict that most practitioners acknowledge in private and minimize in public. The second is a deeper problem that is less often discussed.

The acknowledged conflict is that a firm paid on assets has a structural interest in the continued presence of those assets at the firm. When the question is whether a family should pay down debt, distribute capital to heirs, fund a private operating business, or make a large philanthropic gift, the AUM-compensated firm has a bias against these actions, because each of them reduces the fee base. Most advisors navigate this conflict in good faith. The conflict still exists. A family that understands its own affairs can often detect when advice is being shaded by it.

The deeper problem is different. The AUM fee implies that the primary work of the firm is asset management. For most families at the size we serve, this is not quite right. Asset management is necessary work but it is not, in most years, the work that matters most to the family's continuity. The work that matters most is the drafting of instruments, the coordination of counsel, the management of transitions, the preparation of heirs, and the careful attention to structural decisions that will compound or fail to compound over decades. An AUM fee structurally understates this work because the work is not tied to assets under management. A family with a stable portfolio and a complex ongoing governance situation is paying the same fee as a family with a simpler situation and similar assets. The fee is indifferent to what the firm is actually doing.

The retainer corrects both problems.

The retainer is set by the complexity of the engagement, not by the size of the portfolio. If the family's situation is straightforward, the retainer is lower. If the situation involves multiple trusts, a private operating business, second-generation heirs entering the picture, significant philanthropy, real estate holdings requiring specialized attention, or active succession questions, the retainer is higher. The firm is paid for the work it does.

The retainer is paid annually in advance. This is the traditional structure for professional services in fields like law and medicine, and it has a specific virtue: the firm's income for the year is known at the beginning of the year, which removes the incentive to generate additional billable activity during the year. There is no productivity quota. There is no scramble for a fee-generating decision in the fourth quarter. The firm can do the work the family needs, including the work of doing nothing when nothing is the correct action.

There are some trade-offs. The retainer structure is unfamiliar to most new clients. The first conversation about it usually includes a version of the question, "How do I know the fee is fair?" The honest answer is that the family has the same transparency into our fee as our other clients do, and the retainer can be renegotiated at any annual review if the complexity of the engagement changes. The retainer is also higher than the equivalent AUM fee for small portfolios and lower than the equivalent AUM fee for larger portfolios. This means the structure is partly a filter. Families with assets below a certain threshold find a retainer poorly matched to their needs, which is correct, because the firm is not the right fit for them. Families with assets above a certain threshold find the retainer structurally favorable, which is also correct.

The retainer is not the only honest fee structure, and firms that charge AUM fees are not, as a class, dishonest. The retainer is the structure that best matches what the firm is paid to do. The match is the point.

On a Passage from Burke

A note on inheritance, continuity, and the debts of the living.

18 December 2025

A passage I have been returning to, from Burke's Reflections on the Revolution in France:

"Society is indeed a contract. But the state ought not to be considered as nothing better than a partnership agreement in a trade of pepper and coffee, calico or tobacco, or some other such low concern, to be taken up for a little temporary interest, and to be dissolved by the fancy of the parties. It is to be looked on with other reverence, because it is not a partnership in things subservient only to the gross animal existence of a temporary and perishable nature. It is a partnership in all science, a partnership in all art, a partnership in every virtue and in all perfection. As the ends of such a partnership cannot be obtained in many generations, it becomes a partnership not only between those who are living, but between those who are living, those who are dead, and those who are to be born."

Burke is writing about civil society. The passage applies, I think, to family capital.

Capital that has accumulated across generations is not the property of any one living member of the family. It belongs also to the generations that produced it and to the generations that will inherit it. Those generations are not present at the meetings, but the fiduciary's obligation extends to them anyway. Burke's framing puts it plainly: the partnership is between those who are living, those who are dead, and those who are yet to be born. The living have the capital in their hands. They do not have it for themselves alone.

This is not a mystical claim. It is a description of what inheritance actually is. When a grandparent funds a trust, the grandparent is agreeing to defer consumption to benefit heirs. When the heirs receive the trust, they are receiving a decision made by someone they may never have met. The continuity of the partnership depends on the living treating the capital as partly the property of the dead and partly the property of the unborn.

Most of the structural choices the firm makes with clients are downstream of this framing. The retainer fee, the drafting discipline, the refusal to manage what we do not understand, the long-horizon thinking: these are all small consequences of taking Burke's partnership seriously.

A Letter on Receiving a Referral

What happens between an introduction and a first meeting, and why the interval matters.

2 December 2025

To the reader who is considering an introduction:

When a family is referred to this firm, what follows is rarely what the family expects.

The expectation, usually, is that a referral triggers a sales process. The firm will set up a meeting quickly, send a deck in advance, walk through its capabilities, produce a proposal, and ask for the engagement. This is how most advisory relationships begin in the category, and it is a reasonable thing to expect.

That is not what happens here.

When a referral arrives, I reply within the week. The reply is brief and asks for one thing: a short note, in the referring party's own words, about what has prompted the inquiry. Sometimes this is a specific problem (an impending liquidity event, a trust that needs to be drafted, a family transition). Sometimes it is a general sense that the current arrangement is not serving the family well. Sometimes it is simple curiosity about what a different kind of firm might look like.

That note is how the conversation begins. There is no calendar link. There is no deck.

If the note and the firm's work seem aligned, I propose a first conversation. That conversation is often by phone, sometimes in person if the family is near Fort Wayne or Bond is traveling. It lasts an hour or so. Its purpose is to understand the family's situation in enough detail that I can tell whether the firm is the right fit. If the fit is wrong, I say so and often suggest another firm that would be better suited. This is not rare. It happens in about a third of first conversations.

If the fit is right, a second conversation follows. This one is longer and more specific. By the end of it, the family has a clear picture of what the engagement would look like, what it would cost, and what the first year of work would involve. At no point in this process is there pressure to decide. Most families take several weeks between the second conversation and their decision. Some take longer.

The reason the process is slow is that the work itself is slow. A family office engagement is not a transaction. It is a multi-decade relationship, and the early conversations are the only part of that relationship in which either side can still choose differently. Treating those conversations as a sales process would be a form of dishonesty about the nature of what is being entered into.

I am aware that this approach is unusual and, for some readers, off-putting. A certain kind of family wants speed. They want a yes or no within a week. They want a proposal by email. They want the transaction to feel efficient. Those families are better served by other firms, and the firms that serve them are often good at what they do.

The families who find this firm useful tend to have had the experience of entering a relationship too quickly and regretting it. They want the first conversation to be slow because they understand that the relationship itself will be slow. The pace of the intake is an honest preview of the pace of the work.

On What Stewardship Actually Means

The word appears in the brochure of every wealth management firm. Almost none of them mean the same thing by it.

14 November 2025

Stewardship is the word every firm in this category uses, and it is the word that has been most thoroughly drained of meaning by repetition.

I want to try to recover what it means.

The older use of the word comes from estate management. A steward was the person responsible for the continued productivity of land that was not theirs. The land belonged to the family; the steward worked the land on the family's behalf. A good steward left the land in better condition than he found it. A bad steward extracted from the land and moved on. The stakes were plain, and the role was defined by the fact that the steward did not own the thing he tended.

This older use contains the whole argument. Stewardship is the opposite of ownership. A steward does not act for himself. He acts for the land, for the family that owns it, and for the generations of that family not yet born.

When a wealth management firm uses the word "stewardship" today, it usually means something softer: we will be careful with your money, we will think about the long term, we will not take undue risks. These are good things. They are not stewardship. A firm that charges a percentage of assets under management is structurally oriented toward the continued existence of those assets at the firm. A firm that owns its clients' attention through proprietary products is oriented toward the consumption of those products. These are ownership postures, not steward postures. The firm tends the relationship, but the relationship is its own.

Real stewardship, in the older sense, requires that the firm act on behalf of something other than itself. In the family-office context, the thing being tended is not the portfolio and not the client relationship. The thing being tended is the family's capital across time, including time after the current client has died and the firm's current principal has retired or died as well.

This framing has practical consequences.

A steward will recommend distribution when distribution is correct, even when distribution reduces the steward's fee base. A steward will recommend the sale of a family business when the family has no next-generation operator for it, even if the business represents most of the fee-generating assets. A steward will draft a trust instrument designed to constrain the current client's spending, because the trust is for a grandchild not yet born. A steward will refuse to manage a certain kind of asset because the steward does not understand it well enough to tend it properly, even when managing it would generate fees.

These are difficult postures to maintain. They are also what the word means.

I should be clear that I am not claiming my firm has perfected them. The family office is new. What I can claim is that the structure of the firm has been arranged to remove the most common friction against stewardship. We charge a retainer, not a percentage, so the fee is not linked to continued accumulation. We do not manufacture products. We do not take distribution from the investment firms whose products our clients might hold. The firm's only income is from the client, for the work done on the client's behalf.

This is not unusual in family-office work at the top of the market. It is unusual in the tier we occupy, which is families with real means but not the scale to capitalize a single-family office of their own. That tier has been served, mostly, by firms that use the word "stewardship" in the softer sense. The older sense is available, and the structural arrangements that make it possible are available, and the purpose of the family office is to make them available to a small number of families at that scale.

Whether we succeed at it is a matter of the work, not the marketing. Over a long enough time horizon, the work will reveal whether the word is meant or merely said.
